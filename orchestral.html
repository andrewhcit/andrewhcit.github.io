This is a selection of pieces that Alfie might enjoy listening to. I've included most of the iconic pieces in the classical repertoire and most of the pieces used in popular cartoons or other pop culture (as long as they seem enjoyable throughout for someone new to classical music), added a few personal favorites and other pieces I think are accessible, and tried to sprinkle in some diversity in composers because I don't want it to be limited to dead white men from just six European countries.

I decided to select videos that show the musicians in live performance as long as I could find at least one with both reasonable performance quality and audiovisual recording quality. I considered recording quality at least as important as performance quality in selecting videos; I also tried to limit the amount of non-musical material in the videos (e.g. generally avoiding videos that include over 90 seconds of applause at the end).

Alexander Borodin (1833-1887, Russia) – Overture to Prince Igor
Presidential Orchestra of Belarus / Victor Babarikin
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8yygq0nX8Fs

  • Borodin left his opera Prince Igor unfinished when he suddenly and unexpectedly died of a ruptured aortic aneurysm, and it was completed using his notes by his colleagues Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov and Alexander Glazunov. The overture is one of the greatest feats of musical memory in Western music history, as Borodin had it fully formed in his mind but had written down only a small portion of it. Two weeks before his death, Borodin had informally presented the overture to Rimsky-Korsakov and Glazunov for feedback, playing it on the piano and stopping at many points to describe the orchestration. At the time, of course, neither of the guests would have had any reason to consciously try to remember the overture, and neither heard the overture again after that day – and yet Glazunov reconstructed it in its entirety, his reconstruction being consistent with all the parts that Rimsky-Korsakov remembered.
  • Rimsky-Korsakov and Glazunov did most of the work to complete the opera at Borodin's kitchen table, with the constant distraction of the deceased composer's six cats.

Johannes Brahms (1833-1897, Germany) – Academic Festival Overture
Orchestre de Paris / Paavo Järvi
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=R5pzr5655yw

  • This was Brahms's famous musical joke. Brahms was nominated for an honorary doctorate at the University of Breslau, and accepted it, thinking he would not have to do more than give a few remarks on the occasion. Upon learning that he was expected to compose a piece of music for the occasion, Brahms decided to take a jab at the university by composing an overture based on student drinking songs that were popular at the time.
  • Elmer Bernstein used this piece for his own musical joke in the soundtrack of Animal House: some of the music heard during the title sequence is taken from the Academic Festival Overture, with the same orchestration and only slight changes to the notes. Ironically, the statue of the college's founder appears on screen to the backdrop of an orchestrated drinking song!

  • Sibelius composed Finlandia in 1899 to be played at a gala in Helsinki that was ostensibly to raise money for newspaper pension funds, but also meant to rally support for a free press in Finland, which was then occupied by the Russian Empire.   • You may notice the principal violist in this orchestra playing an unusually-shaped instrument. This is an Iizuka-pattern viola. Developed in the 1970s by a maker named Hiroshi Iizuka, it is one of a number of unusual viola shapes designed in the last century to make larger violas playable by smaller violists. In Iizuka-pattern violas, the narrow, roughly arrow-shaped upper bout allows the violist's hand to reach higher up the fingerboard without being blocked by the body of the instrument.

Darius Milhaud (1892-1974, France) – Le Boeuf sur le Toit
Orchestre de Paris / Alondra de la Parra
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Bv9ii_uc2Rc

  • Milhaud spent two years in Rio de Janeiro as a secretary at the French embassy. For the rest of his life he often drew inspiration from the popular music he had heard there. He composed Le Boeuf sur le Toit in 1920, a year after returning from Brazil. It was originally intended to be the score for a silent Charlie Chaplin film, but when the film was canceled it became a “Cinema Symphony on South American Airs” and then a ballet (“pantomime farce”) set in a Brazilian bar. The piece takes its title from one of the melodies heard in it, a Carnaval tune whose title Milhaud simply translated into French. Years later, a cabaret named after this piece became famous as a gathering place for avant-garde artists.

Lili Boulanger (1893-1918, France) – D'un matin de printemps
Seattle Symphony Orchestra / Cristian Macelaru
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=i4JeeXilFKo

  • Lili Boulanger was the first woman to win the prestigious Prix de Rome for composition, but her career was cut short by her death from tuberculosis at the age of 24. Her symphonic poem D'un matin de printemps was the second-last piece she completed.
  • Lili Boulanger's death may have indirectly created a whole generation of American composers. After she died, her older sister Nadia, also a talented composer, ceased composing and devoted herself entirely to teaching. At the time, there was still a great stigma surrounding American classical musicians in Europe, and Nadia Boulanger was one of the few highly-regarded composition teachers who routinely accepted American students. Her students included a virtual who's-who of American composers active between 1920 and 1970. (Six Degrees of Nadia Boulanger is a thing among American classical musicians today.)

George Gershwin (1898-1937, USA) – An American in Paris
Detroit Symphony Orchestra / Leonard Slatkin
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0PBpOXn3Iqg

  • Gershwin made two trips to Paris in the 1920s to attempt to study there. The first time, in 1926, Maurice Ravel said he could not teach Gershwin, saying, “Why be a second-rate Ravel when you can be a first-rate Gershwin?” The second time, in 1928, he approached Nadia Boulanger only to be turned down again, Boulanger asking him: “What could I give you that you haven't already got?” But Gershwin did not leave Paris entirely empty-handed. He began composing An American in Paris, a symphonic poem portraying the impressions of an American visitor in the city, during his first trip, and completed it shortly after returning from his second trip. For the premiere in New York, Gershwin even purchased and brought home four Parisian taxi horns.
  • Long after Gershwin's death, the musical film An American in Paris was written around Gershwin's music, concluding with an elaborate dance sequence that used the symphonic poem of the same title.

Aaron Copland – El Salón México
Bogotá Philharmonic Orchestra / Luis Fernando Madrid
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=IkBW47soWSw

  • Copland's aim in writing this symphonic poem was not to present “Mexican” music, but to evoke the atmosphere of a particular dance hall in Mexico City. He made several visits to Mexico in the 1930s, and on one of these visits his friend and colleague Carlos Chávez took him to El Salón México. The melodies are derived from popular songs for which Copland purchased sheet music in Mexico City.

Dmitri Shostakovich (1906-1975, Russia) – Festive Overture
Royal Stockholm Philharmonic Orchestra / Yuri Temirkanov
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=1gDZTah8J2A

  • Shostakovich was repeatedly denounced by the Stalin regime for his modernism, and for much of his career he was in such fear of arrest that he kept a packed suitcase under his desk at all times in case he had to flee. He wrote the Festive Overture in just three days, shortly after Stalin's death, and when he conducted it himself it was his first appearance on a concert stage in decades. Not coincidentally, it is one of his few happy-sounding pieces; he was quite clearly dancing on Stalin's grave.
