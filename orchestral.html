This is a selection of pieces that Alfie might enjoy listening to. I've included most of the iconic pieces in the classical repertoire and most of the pieces used in popular cartoons or other pop culture (as long as they seem enjoyable throughout for someone new to classical music), added a few personal favorites and other pieces I think are accessible, and tried to sprinkle in some diversity in composers because I don't want it to be limited to dead white men from just six European countries.

I decided to select videos that show the musicians in live performance as long as I could find at least one with both reasonable performance quality and audiovisual recording quality. I considered recording quality at least as important as performance quality in selecting videos; I also tried to limit the amount of non-musical material in the videos (e.g. generally avoiding videos that include over 90 seconds of applause at the end).

Otto Nicolai (1810-1849, Germany) – Overture to The Merry Wives of Windsor
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=6F9ZuBKDCzY

  • Nicolai was a well-known conductor, and is best known as the Vienna Philharmonic's first-ever conductor. However, his opera The Merry Wives of Windsor was his first and last major success as a composer; he died suddenly of a stroke two months after its premiere. It turns out he could have gotten his big break earlier, but ended up becoming a footnote in opera history instead: in 1841 he turned down an opera libretto that was then offered to a then-unknown Giuseppe Verdi and became Nabucco, the opera that first made Verdi famous.

Jacques Offenbach (1819-1880, France) – Overture to Orpheus in the Underworld
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=JmXYwuBmOuU

  • Offenbach's comic opera Orphée aux enfers (Orpheus in the Underworld) is an irreverent, satirical take on the Orpheus story that skewers Gluck's opera Orfeo ed Euridice, stilted performances of classical drama, and various society and political scandals of the day. Today, it is best known outside classical circles as the origin of the “can-can,” which occurs in the second act of the opera and is also heard near the end of the overture.
