This is a selection of pieces that Alfie might enjoy listening to. I've included most of the iconic pieces in the classical repertoire and most of the pieces used in popular cartoons or other pop culture (as long as they seem enjoyable throughout for someone new to classical music), added a few personal favorites and other pieces I think are accessible (i.e. can be enjoyed purely as a listening experience without much musical knowledge), and tried to sprinkle in some diversity in composers because I don't want it to be limited to dead white men from six European countries.

I decided to select videos that show the musicians in live performance as long as I could find at least one with both reasonable performance quality and audiovisual recording quality. I considered recording quality at least as important as performance quality in selecting videos; I also tried to limit the amount of non-musical material in the videos (e.g. generally avoiding videos that include over 90 seconds of applause at the end).

Alexander Borodin (1833-1887, Russia) – Overture to Prince Igor
Presidential Orchestra of Belarus / Victor Babarikin
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=8yygq0nX8Fs

  • Borodin left his opera Prince Igor unfinished when he suddenly and unexpectedly died of a ruptured aortic aneurysm, and it was completed using his notes by his colleagues Nikolai Rimsky-Korsakov and Alexander Glazunov. The overture is one of the greatest feats of musical memory in Western music history, as Borodin had it fully formed in his mind but had written down only a small portion of it. Two weeks before his death, Borodin had informally presented the overture to Rimsky-Korsakov and Glazunov for feedback, playing it on the piano and stopping at many points to describe the orchestration. At the time, of course, neither of the guests would have had any reason to consciously try to remember the overture, and neither heard the overture again after that day – and yet Glazunov reconstructed it in its entirety, his reconstruction being consistent with all the parts that Rimsky-Korsakov remembered.
  • Rimsky-Korsakov and Glazunov did most of the work to complete the opera at Borodin's kitchen table, with the constant distraction of the deceased composer's six cats.

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=R5pzr5655yw

  • This was Brahms's famous musical joke. Brahms was nominated for an honorary doctorate at the University of Breslau, and accepted it, thinking he would not have to do more than give a few remarks on the occasion. Upon learning that he was expected to compose a piece of music for the occasion, Brahms decided to take a jab at the university by composing an overture based on student drinking songs that were popular at the time.

  • Milhaud spent two years in Rio de Janeiro as a secretary at the French embassy. For the rest of his life he often drew inspiration from the popular music he had heard there. He composed Le Boeuf sur le Toit in 1920, a year after returning from Brazil. It was originally intended to be the score for a silent Charlie Chaplin film, but when the film was canceled it became a “Cinema Symphony on South American Airs” and then a ballet (“pantomime farce”) set in a Brazilian bar. The piece takes its title from one of the melodies heard in it, a Carnaval tune whose title Milhaud simply translated into French. Years later, a cabaret named after this piece became famous as a gathering place for avant-garde artists.

  • Lili Boulanger's death may have indirectly created a whole generation of American composers. At the time, there was still a great stigma surrounding American classical musicians in Europe, and Nadia Boulanger was one of the few highly-regarded composition teachers who routinely accepted American students. Her students included a virtual who's-who of American composers active between 1920 and 1970. (Six Degrees of Nadia Boulanger is a thing among American classical musicians today.)

https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0PBpOXn3Iqg

  • Gershwin made two trips to Paris in the 1920s to attempt to study there. For much of the 20th century, conductors tended to play Beethoven much more slowly for dramatic effect, and assumed that Beethoven's metronome must have been defective. There has been a movement toward taking the metronome marks at face value, which has been controversial. The faster tempo requires a lighter touch on the strings and certainly changes the character of the piece.

Franz Schubert – Symphony No. 9 “Great”
Mannheim Philharmonic Orchestra / Boian Videnoff
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Jo74nDdmjoI

  • Schubert was clearly inspired to write a symphony on an epic scale by his friend and neighbor Beethoven. He begun work on this symphony the year after Beethoven's 9th was first performed, and even quoted fragments of the “Ode to Joy” in its final movement (e.g. 43:02 of video).
  • The nickname “Great” was originally adopted by a publisher because this was the longer of the two symphonies Schubert wrote in C major. The other, his 6th Symphony, became known as the “Little” C major symphony.
  • Both of the orchestras that rehearsed this symphony during Schubert's life canceled their planned performances because the last movement was too difficult for them, and two more attempted performances were similarly abandoned in 1829 and 1836. It was not performed until 1839, more than ten years after Schubert's death, by Felix Mendelssohn's Leipzig Gewandhaus Orchestra, then the leading orchestra in Europe. Even well into the 1840s, major orchestras in Vienna, Paris, and London canceled performances due to inability to play the symphony at an acceptable standard. It was not until the end of the 19th century, when orchestral playing standards had risen significantly, that Schubert's 9th Symphony became widely performed.

  • Niels Gade was Felix Mendelssohn's assistant conductor and then successor as music director of the Leipzig Gewandhaus Orchestra, before going back to Copenhagen and founding the Royal Danish Academy of Music, where he taught almost every noteworthy Scandinavian composer of the second half of the 19th century.
  • How he got there is an interesting story in itself. After he received his engineering degree, he spent a year studying music and performing as a professional cellist before resuming his technical career as a patent examiner.

Dmitry Shostakovich (1906-1975, Russia) – Symphony No. 5
BBC Symphony Orchestra / Valery Gergiev
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=kTqsU7tQW48

  • Shostakovich subtitled this symphony "A Soviet artist's creative response to just criticism," referring to Stalin's denunciation of his recent opera Lady Macbeth of the Mtsensk District and the forced cancellation of his 4th Symphony's premiere. All this took place at the height of Stalin's purges. Shostakovich feared for his life, especially after the high-ranking Red Army general who had been protecting him was himself arrested on trumped-up charges and executed. Shostakovich's "creative response" was a symphony that was more conservative in harmonic language so as to get back into Stalin's good graces, but filled with sarcastic gestures that audiences recognized as a protest: a goose-stepping march in the first movement, a parody of a waltz in the second movement, a third movement echoing the Russian Orthodox requiem, and "forced rejoicing" at the end of the fourth movement as the strings hammer on the same chord 252 times in a row.
  • A description of the end of the symphony, attributed to Shostakovich himself: "The rejoicing is forced, created under threat, as in Boris Godunov. It's as if someone were beating you with a stick and saying, 'Your business is rejoicing, your business is rejoicing,' and you rise, shaky, and go marching off, muttering, 'Our business is rejoicing, our business is rejoicing.'"
