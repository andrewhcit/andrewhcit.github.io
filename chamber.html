Felix Mendelssohn (1809-1847, Germany) – String Octet
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=9JTH4ZdJcjk

  • Mendelssohn's Octet, composed when he was 16, was his first major success and its remarkable maturity is the reason he is considered by some to have been a greater prodigy than Mozart.
  • Mendelssohn was the conductor who brought Bach out of obscurity. This was still years in the future when he composed his Octet, but his lifelong interest in the Baroque era is evident in the fourth movement, which directly quotes a melody from the Hallelujah Chorus in Handel's Messiah.

Antonin Dvorak – Terzetto for Two Violins and Viola
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jsdC5nPoNVk

  • By 1887, the newly-famous Dvorak had quit his jobs as a professional orchestral violist to focus on composition, but was still earning extra money by renting out a room in his house to lodgers. In January of that year, he was delighted to learn that the university student about to move in was an amateur violinist. He composed his Terzetto in a few days, intending to play it with his new lodger and one of his former National Theatra Orchestra colleagues on the two violin parts. The music proved to be too difficult for the young student, so Dvorak wrote an easier set of miniatures for the same instruments. The Terzetto, meanwhile, was published two months later and entered the standard chamber music repertoire almost immediately.

Laura Valborg Aulin (1860-1926, Sweden) – String Quartet No. 1
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtmeec-8EXE (audio only)

  • Laura Valborg Aulin's brother, Tor Aulin, was the founder of one of Europe's first full-time professional string quartets touring as a permanent ensemble. As a result, unusually for a woman composer of the era, her two string quartets were performed all over Europe. They fell into obscurity quite abruptly while she was still alive: after Tor Aulin's quartet disbanded in 1912 and was no longer actively promoting her music, performances of Laura Valborg Aulin's string quartets ceased before the end of the year and they were not heard again until 1991.

Anton Arensky (1861-1906, Russia) – String Quartet No. 2
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=-8LMfvhhahc

  • This quartet bears the inscription “To the memory of Tchaikovsky,” and Arensky used an unusual inverted quartet of one violin, one viola, and two cellos to create the deep sonorities he thought necessary for this homage to the recently-deceased giant of Russian music. The second movement is a set of variations on a theme by Tchaikovsky.
