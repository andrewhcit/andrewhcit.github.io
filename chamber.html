  • Rossini's six string sonatas, for the unusual quartet of two violins, cello, and double bass, are his first compositions, written when he was 12 years old. Antonin Dvorak – Terzetto for Two Violins and Viola +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=jsdC5nPoNVk

  • By 1887, the newly-famous Dvorak had quit his jobs as a professional orchestral violist to focus on composition, but was still earning extra money by renting out a room in his house to lodgers. In January of that year, he was delighted to learn that the university student about to move in was an amateur violinist. He composed his Terzetto in a few days, intending to play it with his new lodger and one of his former National Theatra Orchestra colleagues on the two violin parts. The music proved to be too difficult for the young student, so Dvorak wrote an easier set of miniatures for the same instruments. The Terzetto, meanwhile, was published two months later and entered the standard chamber music repertoire almost immediately.

Laura Valborg Aulin (1860-1926, Sweden) – String Quartet No. 1
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dtmeec-8EXE (audio only)

  • Laura Valborg Aulin's brother, Tor Aulin, was the founder of one of Europe's first full-time professional string quartets touring as a permanent ensemble. As a result, unusually for a woman composer of the era, her two string quartets were performed all over Europe. They fell into obscurity quite abruptly while she was still alive: after Tor Aulin's quartet disbanded in 1912 and was no longer actively promoting her music, performances of Laura Valborg Aulin's string quartets ceased before the end of the year and they were not heard again until 1991.

mvmt 1: https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ok76wUAhato

  • Anton Reicha was a lifelong friend of Beethoven; the two met as teenagers playing in the Bonn orchestra under the direction of Reicha's uncle. They took different paths, though: Reicha, after composing several unsuccessful operas, devoted his life mainly to teaching and is remembered as one of the greatest composition teachers of the 19th century. His students included Franz Liszt, Hector Berlioz, Charles Gounod, Louise Farrenc, and Cesar Franck; Frederic Chopin also looked to him for advice at times.
  • The other thing Reicha is remembered for is popularizing the wind quintet. Though he was not the first to compose for that ensemble, his 25 wind quintets more than doubled the extant wind quintet literature.

Ludwig van Beethoven – Piano Trio No. 7 “Archduke” +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=2Kntf-pwzzU

  • This piece is referred to as the “Archduke” trio because it was composed for Archduke Rudolph of Austria, who was not only a patron but also a piano student of Beethoven, and evidently a virtuoso pianist considering the difficulty of the pieces Beethoven wrote for him. However, the first performance was with the composer himself at the piano. By then Beethoven had lost most of his hearing and played poorly. After two unsuccessful performances of the “Archduke” Trio, Beethoven never appeared as a performer on a concert stage again.
  • The “Archduke” Trio is central in the plot of the Haruki Murakami novel Kafka on the Shore.

Johannes Brahms (1833-1897, Germany) – Piano Trio No. 1 +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=viPLLjpujOU

  • This trio has two dates on it. It was Brahms's first piece of chamber music to be published and performed, when he was 20 years old and still composing under the pseudonym “Johannes Kreisler, Jr.,” and heavily revised in 1889 with as much as a third of the original score deleted and replaced. It is the only later-revised Brahms piece for which the original version survives.
  • For most of the 19th century, music by leading European composers often waited a decade or longer before being performed in the New World. Remarkably, the original version of this trio had its first public performance in the United States. The American pianist William Mason received an early copy of the score while studying in Europe and performed the trio in New York shortly after his return to the United States in 1855.

Antonín Dvořák – Five Bagatelles for two violins, cello, and harmonium +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=0HgNDCCiWxk

  • Dvořák composed his Bagatelles for a small informal chamber music concert at a friend's home. Dvořák performed the piece with the members of his usual string quartet; he normally played the viola, but chose to write a keyboard part for himself instead. The host did not own a piano, but was able to find a portable harmonium (reed organ) for Dvořák to play. Thus the unusual instrumentation was dictated by the people and place involved.

Ernő Dohnányi (1877-1960, Hungary/Germany) – Piano Quintet No. 1 +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=n7RLeE6tHiA

  • Ernő Dohnányi (also known as Ernst von Dohnanyi) made a giant splash with his first published piece, this piano quintet composed when he was 18 years old. However, Dohnanyi was overshadowed as a composer by his Hungarian contemporaries Bartók and Kodály largely because he was a mainstream late Romantic composer rather than a nationalist. He was known mainly as a leading conductor during his life, and is probably best known today as the grandfather of conductor Christoph von Dohnanyi (Cleveland Orchestra) and German politician Klaus von Dohnanyi (former West German Minister of Education and mayor of Hamburg).

Franz Schubert (1797-1828, Austria) – Arpeggione Sonata (viola transcription) +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=v8MMM42yU4Y

  • This is the only significant piece ever composed for the arpeggione, an instrument invented in the early 19th century that was supposed to be a hybrid of the guitar and cello. The arpeggione had a cello-like body and was played with a bow, but it had six strings tuned to the same notes as a guitar and its fingerboard had frets. Fewer than a hundred arpeggiones have ever been made, most of them since 1980.
  • Schubert's Arpeggione Sonata has instead become a staple of the viola repertoire and is also occasionally played by cellists. The viola version transposes many passages an octave higher.

Alexander Borodin (1833-1887, Russia) – Cello Sonata
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=39uLX6M6Gn4

  • This sonata was unfinished at Borodin's death, and completed using Borodin's sketches nearly a hundred years later.
  • The cello was Borodin's own primary instrument, and the style of the piece suggests that the intended performer was Borodin himself. It is very much a fusion of eras: it is unmistakably Romantic, but the entire piece is built on the quotation from Bach that begins it, and many of the more virtuosic passages are reminiscent of Boccherini, whose chamber music Borodin loved to play.

Johannes Brahms – “F-A-E” Scherzo +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=3MreemUwbHE

  • A young Johannes Brahms composed this scherzo as his contribution to the “F-A-E Sonata,” a collaboration between him, Robert Schumann, and Schumann's student Albert Dietrich. The sonata was a gift to violinist Joseph Joachim, a friend of all three composers. Because Joachim used the Romantic German phrase “Frei aber einsam” (free but lonely) as a personal motto, the composers based each movement on the sequence of notes F-A-E. The complete piece was presented to Joachim and performed privately, but not published or performed in public until 1935, long after all three composers and Joachim had died. The two movements Schumann composed were eventually incorporated into his Violin Sonata No. 3, Brahms's movement was published as a stand-alone piece, and Dietrich never reused or published his movement separately from the F-A-E Sonata.

Amanda Maier (1853-1894, Sweden/Netherlands) – Violin Sonata +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=hMwIl8uwGVE

  • This sonata, one of my favorites, is closely connected with Brahms's third violin sonata. Maier became part of Brahms's social circle while a student of violin and composition in Leipzig, and Brahms considered her to be a first-rate composer. Maier's violin sonata and Brahms's third violin sonata were begun around the same time, and both were composed slowly over a period of years with the two composers exchanging drafts for feedback. Both sonatas were eventually performed in Amsterdam with Maier on violin and Brahms on piano. After Maier died of tuberculosis at a relatively young age, her music was largely forgotten and this sonata is believed to have gone 100 years (1894-1994) between performances.

Modern:

Rebecca Clarke (1886-1979, England/USA) – Viola Sonata +
https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=AtwjlUCQPgY

  • Rebecca Clarke is obscure to the general public, but virtually all violists know her name; one of the reasons for her obscurity is that so much of her music featured solo viola! She was one of the first six women to play in a major professional orchestra when the Queen's Hall Orchestra opened auditions to women in 1912; four years later she moved to the United States (already a citizen through her American father) and became America's first full-time touring viola soloist just as the viola was beginning to come into its own as a solo instrument.
  • Clarke's viola sonata tied for first prize in an international competition in 1919, but fell into obscurity after Clarke retired from performing in the 1940s. It was revived beginning in the 1970s while she was still alive. Since 1990, it has been the single most performed piece for solo viola, making the viola unique among the instruments of the orchestra in having its most-performed solo work composed by a woman.
